Walking the Greenway: Urban Nature and You

By Simon Oszust — September 1, 2025

The urban greenway is more than a strip of plants and a paved trail — it's a breathing space inside the city, a corridor where people, birds, and small mammals pass through the daily noise. On weekend mornings I take a sixty-minute loop that starts at the river bridge and winds past community gardens, a playground, and a little wetland that many people overlook. The ritual of walking turns into a slow study of details: the angle of light through leaves, the sound of different shoes on the path, the way a dog pauses at the same tree every time. It matters because these details knit together a sense of belonging and well-being. Studies have shown (and common sense confirms) that regular exposure to pockets of nature reduces stress and sharpens attention. You don't need a perfect park — only an accessible clean path where sightlines allow you to relax and watch life go by.

On my walks I try small experiments in observation: one week I track bird species I see, another I notice what plants are blooming. These micro-projects shift the walk from background exercise to active curiosity. They also make the greenway an educational resource for neighborhoods: schools can adopt sections for cleanup, local artists can install temporary sculptures, and volunteers can map invasive plants. When people invest a little time, the greenway becomes a social thread that ties adjacent blocks together. If you're new to urban walking, start slowly. Bring a small notebook or use your phone to jot what you notice. Over time you'll notice how the same path looks different in rain, fog, or late summer heat — and you'll begin to feel the subtle rhythm of your city.

Accessibility matters, too. Make sure paths are clear of overgrown bushes and have safe crossings at busy streets. Advocate for benches spaced so older walkers can rest. A well-kept greenway supports everyone from joggers to grandparents with strollers. Every city deserves a well-loved, well-maintained green corridor — and if your city doesn't have one yet, maybe you can start the conversation with a neighbor or community group.

Home Coffee Brewing: Simple Gear, Big Flavor

By Simon Oszust — August 18, 2025

Brewing better coffee at home doesn't require a lab of equipment. With a few thoughtful choices and consistent technique you can raise your morning cup by leaps. My basic checklist is: fresh whole beans, a burr grinder, good water (filtered if your tap is very hard), a scale that measures grams, and a brewing method that matches your patience level. For many people, a simple pour-over dripper (V60-style) or an immersion brewer (like a French press) gives a wide range of pleasant results without a steep learning curve. The key is controlling two main variables: grind size and brew ratio. Too fine a grind makes the coffee bitter; too coarse makes it weak.

Below is a small reference table I use while testing roast and grind settings. It's simple and practical — feel free to adapt it to your taste. The table shows three example recipes for different flavor intensity targets.

Method Coffee (g) Water (g)
Light / Bright (pour-over) 18 300
Balanced (pour-over) 20 300
Bold / Full (French press) 30 450

Beyond numbers, tasting is essential. Adjust your grind a notch and re-taste. If the brew is sour, it may be under-extracted (try finer grind or longer contact time). If it's overly bitter, try a coarser grind or slightly cooler water (about 92–96°C is a good range). Freshness of beans is also huge — coffee is usually best in the first two to four weeks after roast. Buy smaller amounts and grind right before brewing.

Finally, don't let gear be an excuse to procrastinate. A solid manual kettle and a reliable grinder will pay off more than flashy gadgets. Above all, enjoy the ritual: time your pours, note batch differences, and lean into the sensory experience. Coffee is a daily window into practice and pleasure.
